College Football: A Battle Against Conference Realignment

A brief history of college football and one of its biggest obstacles, including one that looms larger now than ever: conference realignment.

The Stages of Realignment

To understand how we got here, we must look at the specific moves made by the "Power" conferences:

  1. Phase 1: The First Ripples (1990s - 2005)
    • The SEC creates the first Conference Championship game, sparking the need for more members.
    • The ACC raids the Big East for Miami and Virginia Tech to bolster its football profile.
  2. Phase 2: The Early 2010s Shift
    • Nebraska leaves the Big 12 for the Big Ten, ending a century of regional tradition.
    • Texas A&M and Missouri join the SEC, cementing the SEC as a massive media powerhouse.
    • The Big East collapses as a football power, leading to the "American" (AAC) conference.
  3. Phase 3: The Modern "Super-Conference" Era (Present Day)
    • The "Big Two" (SEC and Big Ten) begin massive expansion, adding USC, UCLA, and Texas.
    • The Pac-12—the "Conference of Champions"—effectively dissolves as members flee for stability.
    • Media rights deals reach the billion-dollar threshold, prioritizing TV markets over geography.

The Major Conflict: Revenue vs. Tradition

The primary issue facing college football today is the death of regionality. For over a century, the sport was defined by neighbors playing neighbors, allowing fans to easily travel to away games and maintaining decades-old rivalries.

Now, with teams like Washington playing at Rutgers, or Stanford traveling to Syracuse, the travel burden on student-athletes has skyrocketed. Furthermore, the "revenue gap" is widening; as the Big Ten and SEC sign massive billion-dollar television deals, smaller conferences are being left behind, creating a two-tiered system that threatens the very fabric of the "any given Saturday" spirit.

The Spirit of the Game

As fans we like to think that pure enjoyment of the sport is what reigns supreme.
